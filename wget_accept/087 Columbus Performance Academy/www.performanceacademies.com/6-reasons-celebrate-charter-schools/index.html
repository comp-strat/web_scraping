6 Reasons to Celebrate Charter Schools

05-5-2014 | Myrrha Satow

It is fitting that National Charter Schools Week and Teacher Appreciation Week fall during the same week this year. President Obama declared this week National Charter Schools Week, May 4-10, 2014.  Parents, students, and community members owe a great deal to successful charter schools, charter school pioneers, and charter school teachers. Arguably, the most important reason to celebrate charter schools is they offer educational choice to families otherwise restricted to a failing school because of their zip code.  Fortunately, because of early charter school pioneers and supporters, there are now over 2.5 million students enrolled in more than 6,000 charter schools nationally (Source: National Association of Public Charter Schools, www.public charters.org). One in twenty students now attends a public charter school. More reasons to support and celebrate charter schools this week and always are below:

1) Charter schools empower parents and students. Children should not be limited to a failing school because of their zip code.  Where a child lives should not determine whether a child receives a quality education.  Charters offer parents educational choice and educational mobility. Charters empower families.

2) One size does not fit all; different kids need different schools.  Many traditional public schools do a decent job of educating most of their kids.  But many schools don’t, and even in high performing school districts, there are children who suffer academically, socially, emotionally, through bullying, or for other reasons. Charter schools provide a unique programmatic opportunity for students who were otherwise not always successful in their previous school and for parents who were dissatisfied in their child’s previous school. After all, if parents were satisfied with their previous school, they wouldn’t enroll their child in a charter school.

3) Achievement outcomes are higher in many charter schools. School choice improves student achievement. Countries like Finland, Belgium, and Australia, where parents actively choose their schools have better schools. (See “Education Myths”, Jay P. Greene).

4) Charter schools often provide smaller class sizes, and equally important, smaller school sizes.  Parents care about strong academics, but they care about other important factors as well.  These include safety, opportunities to be involved in their child’s education, frequent communication between home and school, and more. What smaller class sizes and smaller school sizes mean to parents are often more opportunities for parent involvement, better home and school communication, and more opportunities to focus on other facets arguably as important as academic achievement. Such factors may include character education, anti-bullying programs, school safety, niche programs (such as music, art, fitness, or technology), greater access to teachers and administrators, and stronger school/family relationships.  Moreover, parents report real and tangible benefits related to the fact that nearly everyone in the building may know their particular child, including the school administrators, support staff, and classroom teachers.

5) Charter schools are a bargain to the American taxpayer; charter schools do much more with less money.  Charter schools efficiently deliver quality education to students with fewer resources. In 2011, charters received $3059 less per student than traditional public schools (in urban districts, that figure is much higher), and that disparity has grown by 55% since 2007.  All states, except for Tennessee, gave more money to traditional public schools than charter schools (See: University of Arkansas Dep. of Ed Reform study). Charter schools are more economical to the taxpayer, and they are more efficient.  Most charters are unable to access local tax levy revenue like the local districts, and most receive significantly fewer dollars per pupil to educate students. Most importantly, children were never meant to receive an education through the churning of huge district wheels designed to crank out thousands of kids.  The necessary and associated burdens of huge administrative overhead costs take away from classrooms. Children are not widgets. The layers and layers of bureaucracy, policies, and systems designed to meet the needs of thousands of kids in a district or a building for a thousand students make no sense.

6) Charter schools are bound by the ultimate accountability consequence–closure for poor academic outcomes tied to a limited period of time. Many behemoth, failing, traditional public school districts continue to produce high drop out rates, students who can’t read by the third grade, and poor overall academic results for kids.  Many of these schools are not safe from bullying or threats. How long should that be permitted to continue?  Should traditional districts be given two years to get it together before they close like many charters schools? Should they be given 5 years, 10 years?  What is our tolerance for public schools with no consequence for academic failure? Many have had poor outcomes for decades with no real consequence that sticks.  Charter schools are held to the highest level of accountability for poor academic outcomes. They actually have their funding taken away, and they actually close for poor performance.  More importantly, we are seeing “loft” in the charter community over time as quality charter schools continue to rise and provide more quality options for parents and students.

National Charter Schools Week 2014 is indeed a great time to recognize the power and opportunities inherent in charter schools for parents and students. With one million students on charter school waiting lists nationwide, it is clear parents desire educational choice.  Celebrate your charter school and your charter school teachers today!
